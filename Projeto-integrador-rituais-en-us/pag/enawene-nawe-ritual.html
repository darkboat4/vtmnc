ENAWENÊ-NAWÊ

Yaokwa

The Yaokwa marks the dry season, during which the Enauênes-Nauês interact with beings from the underworld called Yakairiti. As these creatures suffer from insatiable hunger, they need to be fed with vegetable salt, fish, corn, and cassava. In exchange for the food they receive, the spirits maintain social and cosmic order.

The preparation for Yaokwa begins in January, the time of cassava harvest, and when the Mata is constructed—a series of fishing traps along the Joaquim Rios, Arimena, Rio Preto, and Nhambiquara rivers. During the fishing expedition, the village divides into nine ritual groups according to clans. The fishermen, having removed the ornaments that identify them as human and grouped into teams, then set out to camp along the banks of medium-sized rivers.

After two months of fishing, at each dam, an elder offers salt to a group of fishermen (representing the Yakairiti) in exchange for the fish. The consumption of salt symbolizes the pact between humans and spirits. Upon returning to the village, the fishermen, once again representing beings from the underworld, face the residents who have been tending to the fields. After the ritual songs, they exchange fish for drinks prepared with cassava and corn.
